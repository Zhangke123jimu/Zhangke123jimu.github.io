This project evaluated the long-term cost-effectiveness of a bispecific antibody compared with a standard-of-care regimen for 
adults with relapsed or refractory hematologic malignancy. The analysis used a three-state partitioned survival model with progression-free survival, progressed disease, and death states to simulate lifetime costs and quality-adjusted life-years(QALYs) from the Chinese healthcare system perspective.

As a core researcher, my work focused on model adaptation, parameter implementation, survival curve extrapolation, and uncertainty analysis. 
I used R to perform parametric survival extrapolation by fitting standard parametric survival models to PFS and OS time-to-event data, incorporated clinical efficacy, utility, cost, and treatment-duration inputs into an Excel-based model, and used VBA to automate model outputs, deterministic sensitivity analysis, tornado plots, probabilistic sensitivity analysis, and scenario analyses.

The model compared lifetime costs, QALYs, and cost-effectiveness results under base-case and alternative scenario assumptions. 
Sensitivity analyses were used to identify key drivers of model uncertainty and to assess the robustness of the cost-effectiveness conclusion.
